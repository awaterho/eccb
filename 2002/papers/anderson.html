Comparative genomics of microbial pathogens and symbionts
Siv Andersson, Uppsala University, Uppsala, Sweden
Siv G.E. Andersson, Cecilia Alsmark, Carolin Frank, Olof Karlberg, Lisa Klasson, Boris Antoine-Legault, Ivica Tamas Department of Molecular Evolution, Evolutionary Biology Center, University of Uppsala, Uppsala, Sweden We are interested in quantifying the contribution of lateral gene transfer, innovation, loss, expansion and rearrangements to the evolution of microbial genomes. Here, we discuss the results of pair-wise genome comparisons of closely related species, including our own recently obtained genome sequence data. We compare architectural divergence to nucleotide sequence divergence for microbes with different lifestyles. A particular focus is on comparisons of the recently obtained 1.6 Mb and 1.9 Mb genome sequences of Bartonella henselae and Bartonella quintana, the vector-borne agents of cat-scratch disease and trench fever, respectively. We show that the B. quintana genome is a smaller subset of the B. henselae genome. As observed for many pathogens, the main difference between closely related strains and species resides in the content of phage genes and pathogenicity islands. This flexibility in genome structure contrasts with the extreme stability observed in the small genomes of aphid endosymbionts, in which no rearrangements or inflow of genetic material have occurred during the past 50 millions years (1). We discuss the results of large-scale phylogenomic analyses of these genomes and their relationships to free-living bacteria and mitochondria. Our data suggests that the degree of genomic stability correlate with the genomic content of repeated sequences and movable genetic elements, and thereby with bacterial lifestyle.
