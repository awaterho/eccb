Poster H01
Photoperception in Halobacterium salinarum: a systems biology approach.
Cangelosi D. (1), Cercignani G. (4,3), Chiarugi D. (2), Felicioli C. (1), Freschi L. (1), Fulgentini L. (5,3), Marangoni R. (1,3)
(1) Dipartimento di Informatica, Universita' di Pisa, Italy., (2) Dipartimento di Matematica e Informatica, Universita' di Siena, Italy., (3) Istituto di Biofisica del CNR, Pisa, Italy., (4) Dipartimento di Biologia, Universita' di Pisa, Italy., (5) Dipartimento di Scienze dell'Ambiente e della Vita, Universita' del Piemonte Orientale "Amedeo Avogadro", Italy.
The photoperception signaling pathway in Halobacterium salinarum is a multi-scale system, which confers the bacterium a very complex behavior in response to different irradiation regimes. We developed a stochastic simulation of such signaling pathway. In the present work we detailed the simulation strategies, along with the harvesting strategies of biological data. We investigated the accord between the real and the simulated systems: the discrepancies are explained by proposing alternative hypotheses, the biological reliability of which is discussed in detail.
One of the hot themes in contemporary systems biology is the description of signaling pathways. Differently from the usually simulated metabolic pathways, like glycolysis, in which the metabolites have always comparable concentrations and the reactions show comparable kinetic constants, often the reactions involved in signaling pathways show huge differences in kinetic constants and intermediates concentrations. This implies that the simulation approach is non trivial. In this work we focused our interest on a specific signaling pathway: the photoperception in Halobacterium salinarum. It belongs to Archea and can swim by means of some flagella located in the distal region of the cell body. It is able to move forwards and backwards, dependently on the flagella rotation direction: clockwise and counterclockwise, respectively. The switch between forwards to backwards is called "reversal", and takes place spontaneously with a quasi-regular frequency (spontaneous reversal frequency, SRF). H. salinarum can also respond to external stimuli by altering the reversal frequency (RF): an attractive stimulus decreases RF, whereas a repellent stimulus increases it. With respect to similar signaling pathways, e.g. the chemotaxis, photoperception is more interesting because it offers a wide spectrum of behavioral responses, in dependence of different characteristics of the actinic light (spectral composition, intensity, orientation, gradient, polarity, etc.). In particular, H. salinarum, responds in different ways to different spectral bands: for example, orange light acts as an attractant, while blue light acts as a repellent. The simultaneous exposure to both the bands can give rise to very complex behavior. Therefore, the in-silico model can be tested against a large variety of experimental situations. The first step of simulation activity was to design the topology of photoperception signaling pathway: here we summoned the results published in the literature. H. salinarium show two rhodopsins which are responsible for photoreception: Sensory rhodopsin I and Sensory rhodopsin II. Both these pigments show a photocycle: i.e., they change absorption peak, once they are excited by a photon. The photocycle can have several intermediate states, and, therefore, the response of the molecule to polychromatic irradiation can be very complex. Apart from the early steps involving the photon absorption, the consequent transduction chain is relatively simple, and very close to that of chemotaxis in Escherichia coli. Since no experimental data are available for the kinetic values of most of the reactions in H. salinarium photoreception transduction chain, we have imported, where possible, the data from the chemotactic transduction chain of E. coli. The poster will detail the complete list of reactions, specifying the kinetic coefficients used, providing the reference of their source and the justification of eventual modifications or arbitrary assignations. We refer to this model as "ground model", since it attempts to take into account all the data known from the literature. It can be arranged into three main blocks: the proper photoreception and the early signal transfer, by the receptor-transductor complex; the phosphorylation block, concerning some intermediates, the biological activity of which changes dependently on their phosphorylation state; the methylation block, which show a large interaction with the phosphorylation block, since the propensity to be methylated or demethylated changes dependingly also on the phosphorylation state (and vice versa). There is also a fourth block, represented by the flagellar motor: it has 4 different binding sites that the last intermediate of the transduction chain can occupy, only when it is in its phosphorylated status: when more than 3 sites are occupied, the flagellar motor switches and the virtual bacterium makes a reversal. We can, therefore, establish a direct link between the simulated system and the real behavioral measurements, using the light energy as input and the RF as output of the model. The time evolution of the model was simulated by using the well-known Direct Method proposed in 1977 by Gillespie, in particular we implemented the fastest exact version of it, called Logarithmic Direct Method. Nevertheless, we have, where possible, approximated or modified the description of some reactions in order to make them fully compatible with Gillespie's algorithm. These approximations have been chosen to keep preserved their biological reliability. We run several simulations of such model, aimed at testing the behavior of the simulated systems under different irradiation regimes, experimented on the real bacterium and described in the literature. We focused our interest on four different regimes: 1. the dark 2. a continuous orange light; 3. a continuous blue light 4. flashes of blue light under a continuous orange light. We firstly explored the qualitative accord between the behavior of the simulated system and that of the real one. We found several discrepancies under the different regimes: we used the model as hypotheses test instrument and we have screened different possible explanations that take into account: - possible incompleteness in the pathway topology (i.e., some reactions not yet described); - possible incorrectness in kinetic coefficient measurements - possible divergence between E.coli chemotaxis and H.salinarum phototaxis transduction chains, which makes false the hypothesis to import values measured in E.coli for simulating H.salinarum phototaxis. Focusing our interest on the pathway main blocks and deducing some minimal hypotheses to recover a qualitative correspondence with the real behavior, we selected three hypotheses which can account for the observed discrepancies, and have a biological reliability. We propose them for a further experimental investigation.
Keywords: Signaling pathways, photoperception, Halobacterium salinarum, Gillespie's algorithm
Poster H02
FUNCTIONAL Module Identification through Protein Interaction Network Alignment
Ali W, Deane CM
Department of Statistics , University of Oxford , 1 South Parks Road , Oxford , OX1 3TG , United Kingdom
Network alignment can be used to identify conserved sub-graphs in protein networks across species. These identified motifs can then be used to transfer annotation in a wide variety of ways. We investigated the use of functional similarity for alignment of protein networks from two species and found the results comparable to sequence alignment. More biological similarity measures could enable much better alignment.
Recent proliferation of protein interaction network data for a number of species has paved the way for using interaction data for functional and structural annotation of proteins. Network alignment can be used to identify conserved sub-graphs in protein networks across species. The premise when applying this technique is that the motifs must have functional importance. As such these identified motifs can then be used to transfer annotation in a wide variety of ways, within the modules: both inter and intra-species. Most existing network alignment programs use sequence similarity exclusively to identify homologous proteins in different species. We are investigating the possibility of using various other biological similarity measures to produce better results. In particular we have used functional similarity for alignment of two networks from S.Crevisiae and H.Sapiens. The interaction data was taken from the Database of Interacting Proteins. Gene Ontology annotations were used for assignment of function to the proteins in each species. A similarity score between each pair of proteins was calculated, that takes into account the specificity of their annotation terms. The results we obtained show that the modules extracted by using functional similarity give slightly better scores than sequence based alignment, in terms of their overlap with identified protein complexes in MIPS, as well as in terms of inter-module functional homogeneity. Moreover, function based alignment produced a surprisingly high number of novel modules which were not identified by the sequence based approach. Thus, combining these two measures may reveal much more information about the modular structure of protein networks. We are building upon this idea to design alignment techniques capable of not only aligning multiple networks, but also using multiple available data sources of biological similarity between proteins.
Keywords: proteins,networks,alignment,modules
Poster H03
Discovering Geometric Structure in Protein-Protein Interaction Networks: The Embedding Algorithm
Desmond J. Higham (1), Marija Rasajski (2,3), Natasa Przulj (2)
(1) Department of Mathematics, University of Strathclyde, Glasgow G1 1XH, UK, (2) Department of Computer Science, UC Irvine, Irvine, CA, USA, (3) Faculty of Electrical Engineering, University of Belgrade, Belgrade, Serbia
Finding a good network null model for protein-protein interaction (PPI) networks is of great importance. It has been proposed that geometric random graphs are a good model for PPI networks. To verify this we design an algorithm that embeds a network into a low dimensional Euclidean space. The algorithm is based on Multi-Dimensional Scaling, with pathlength playing the role of the distance. It exploits sparsity for computational efficiency. We have tested the algorithm on 19 PPI networks of various organisms. The results yield support to the hypothesis that PPI networks are indeed geometric.
Introduction Networks (also called graphs) are widely used to describe natural phenomena explored in computational and systems biology. Nodes in networks represent biomolecules such as genes, proteins, or metabolites, and edges connecting the nodes indicate functional, physical, or chemical interactions between them. Understanding, summarizing and extracting information from these networks are key goals in systems biology. Constructing a good network null model for protein-protein interaction (PPI) networks is a fundamental issue, since such a model would provide insight into the biological structure and function and could be used as a guide for further biological experiments. It has been established that random geometric graphs give an excellent fit for various global and local measures of PPI network structure such as pathlengths, clustering coefficients, relative graphlet frequencies [1], and graphlet degree distributions [2]. We design an algorithm to test directly whether PPI networks are geometric by embedding them into a low dimensional Euclidean space. If a geometric network model fits the PPI data, then it is expected that PPI networks could be embedded into the Euclidean space. The algorithm is based on Multi-Dimensional Scaling, with pathlengths playing the role of the Euclidean distances. We judge the sensitivity and specificity of the fit by computing the areas under the Receiver Operator Characteristic (ROC) curve. We test the algorithm on 19 PPI networks of various organisms. The results yield support to the hypothesis that PPI networks are geometric. The Algorithm Geometric random graphs in 2-dimensional Euclidean space are generated in the following way: we place N nodes uniformly at random in the unit square, and two nodes are connected by an edge if they are within a given Euclidean distance. The 3- and 4-dimensional cases are defined analogously. Our task is to embed the proteins into n-dimensional Euclidean space given only their PPI network connectivity information. The new algorithm consists of the following steps: - Define a pairwise distance d(i,j) between each pair of proteins i and j as the square root of their pathlength, unless this value exceeds a prescribed cutoff. In the latter case, set the distance to some value Kmax. - Double center the distance information, giving a symmetric positive semi-definite matrix A. - Compute v1, v2, ..., vn, the n dominant eigenvalues of A, using the subspace iteration algorithm. - Give each protein i coordinates in n-dimensional Euclidean space from the i-th coordinates of the eigenvectors v1, ..., vn. - Each possible choice of radius now corresponds to a geometric graph. By varying the radius, measure specificity and sensitivity and record the overall goodness of fit by computing the area under the ROC curve. Results The algorithm is applied to 19 real-world PPI networks of 4 eucaryotic organisms, as well as to artificial networks generated using 7 types of random graph models. We analyze 9 yeast S. cerevisiae, 1 fruitfly D. melanogaster, 3 worm C. elegans, and 6 human PPI networks from different studies. We embed these networks into 2D, 3D and 4Dspace. The resulting areas under the ROC curve (AUC) are 0.8924, 0.8934, 0.8957 corresponding to the embeddings of the yeast high-confidence (YHC) [4] network into 2D, 3D and 4D space. The AUCs for the remaining PPI networks are between 0.6207 and 0.8651. We focus our further analyses on YHC network. We construct random graphs corresponding to YHC network (i.e., having the same number of nodes and edges as YHC network) using the following random graph models: Erdos-Renyi random graphs, random graphs with the same degree distribution as the data, d-dimensional geometric random graphs (GEO-dD) for d=2, 3, 4, scale-free Barabasi-Albert networks, and stickiness-index based networks (see [5] for details). For each of these random graph models, we generate 30 model networks corresponding to YHC network, embed them into 2-D, 3-D and 4-D space and compute the means and the standard deviations of the AUCs. For geometric random networks we get means between 0.9055 and 0.9867. For non-geometric networks we get means between 0.6465 and 0.7803. All standard deviations are O(10^-2) or lower. PPI networks are noisy. Thus, to test whether PPI networks have a geometric structure, we add noise to GEO-3D networks by randomly rewiring 10%, 20% and 30% of their edges. We generate 30 networks for each of these re-wiring regimes, embed them into 2D, 3D and 4D space and compute their AUCs. The mean values of AUCs for the 10% rewired GEO-3D networks are 0.81098, 0.87491, and 0.91779, corresponding to 2D, 3D and, 4D space, respectively. This suggests structural similarity between noisy GEO-3D networks and YHC network. Conclusion We provide a method for embedding networks into low dimensional Euclidean space. The method provides a direct test of whether PPI networks have a geometric graph structure. Our results yield support to the hypothesis that the structure of currently available PPI networks is consistent with the structure of noisy geometric graphs. This work has been published in [3]. [1] Przulj et al., Bioinformatics, 20(18): 3508-15, 2004. [2] Przulj, Bioinformatics, 23(2):e177-83, 2006. [3] Higham et al., Bioinformatics 24(8):1093-9, 2008. [4] von Mering et al., Nature,417(6887):399-403, 2002. [5] Przulj and Higham, J. Roy. Soc. Interface, 3(10):711-716, 2006.
Keywords: interactome, random graph models, multi-dimensional scaling, sparse matrix computations, spectral algorithms
Poster H04
An Efficient Method for Identifying Statistical Interactors in Graphical Models
Alina Andrei, Christina Kendziorski
Department of Biostatistics and Medical Informatics, University of Wisconsin-Madison, 1300 Univ. Ave, Madison, Wisconsin, 53703, U.S.A.
Network reconstruction is a main goal of many biological endeavors. Graphical Gaussian models (GGMs) are often employed as an effective way to summarize gene associations. However,GGMs are limited in that interactions are not accommodated. When applied in the presence of interactions, the GGM framework can lead to incorrect inference regarding dependence. We present a computationally efficient approach to identify interactions in networks. Simulations suggest that the method works well, even in fairly complicated scenarios. The approach is applied on microarray data from a breast cancer study.
The reconstruction of biological networks is a key component of efforts to better understand, diagnose, and treat disease. With advances in high throughput technologies, the data necessary for reconstruction are now available and so too are a variety of statistical reconstruction methods (see Hartemink, Nature Biotechnology 2005; Werhli et al., Bioinformatics 2006). Graphical Gaussian models (GGMs) are often used since the underlying assumptions are well understood, edges are easy to interpret, and estimation algorithms are readily available (Schafer and Strimmer, Bioinformatics 2005a, SAGMB 2005b; Li and Gui Biostatistics 2006; Yuan and Lin, Biometrika 2007). In short, nodes are assumed to follow a multivariate normal (MVN) distribution and an edge represents conditional dependence between two nodes, given all other nodes. Thus, the estimation of the graph is equivalent to estimation of the partial correlation (paCor) matrix. This simple, yet effective, framework has proven useful in a number of genomic studies (Toh and Horimoto, Bioinformatics 2002; Wille et al., Genome Biology 2004; Ma et al., Genome Research 2007; Keller et al., Genome Research 2008). In spite of the clear advantages, GGMs are limited in that they do not account for multiplicative dependence among nodes, due to the underlying MVN assumption. This limitation is an important one, since interactions are common in biological studies of individual complex traits (Wittenburg et al., Physiological Genomics 2001; Carlborg et al., Genome Research 2003), studies of groups of metabolites (Wentzell et al., PLoS Genetics 2007), and high-throughput studies of gene expression (Kirst et al., Genetics 2005; Brem and Kruglyak PNAS 2005; West et al., Genetics 2007). In practice, one often proceeds with GGM estimation and inference in the presence of interactions. A simple motivating example presented in this paper shows that, doing so can lead to incorrect inference regarding dependence. Inferring the correct dependence structure is challenging when interactions are present,since an analogue of the paCor matrix that completely determines the dependence structure is not available, and dependencies can involve many nodes. The difficulty is exacerbated for studies with a large number of nodes (p) and relatively small sample size (n). Cox and Wermuth (Applied Statistics, 1994) recognized this problem. In an investigation of the possible reasons for departures from MVN, they focused on the presence of pairwise interactions, noting concern "that the reduction of the observations to covariance matrices overlooks important features of the dependences of intrinsic interest". They proposed an approach to identify the interactions that evaluates all possible linear regression models among the variables considered allowing for single pairwise interaction terms. Interactions with large t-statistics are identified as important and considered for further empirical investigation. Although useful when few nodes are available, the approach cannot be easily scaled to cases with large p, and is not possible when n is smaller than p. As in Cox and Wermuth, we too consider the special case of non-MVN where the full conditional distributions are Gaussian, but the joint is not, due to pairwise interactions. Within this context, we propose a computationally efficient approach that uses GGM estimation techniques to identify variables affected by interactions, and thereby identify regions in a resulting graph where edges may not imply direct or linear dependencies. A key element is recognizing that interactions have a marginal linear effect, and as a result, information about their presence can be obtained from the paCor matrix. Theoretical derivations for the exact effect motivate the approach. The method relies on estimation of multiple paCor matrices, and as a result is only slightly more computationally demanding than traditional GGM reconstruction. Simulation studies show that the approach identifies interactors with high sensitivity for practical levels of heritability and moderate sample sizes, including cases where n is smaller than p, while controlling the FDR. We applied our approach to microarray data from a study of breast cancer, where we identify novel interactors. The graph derived reveals information regarding direct dependencies (additive or multiplicative effects), and perhaps more importantly, highlights variables that warrant further investigation, namely those involving interactions. As a way to further investigate the interactors found, we fitted local linear regression models with and without the interaction term. 69% of the interactors detected with our method were also found significant with regression models (pvalue<0.05). The interaction models resulted in comparably lower BICs, and heritabilities of expresssion traits were significantly increased when accounting for interactors. Supporting plots reinforcing the presence of interactors are shown. In summary, we have presented an approach to identify statistical interactions in gene association networks. The main advantages compared with other network reconstruction approaches that allow for non-linear relationships among nodes (e.g. Friedman et al., J.of Comp. Biology 2000) are that the approach makes relatively few, but testable, assumptions, does not rely on prior specification, and is computationally efficient, requiring little increase in computation over traditional GGM fitting. Extensions to more general networks are underway and should prove useful particularly for network reconstructions involving continuous phenotype and discrete genotype data.
Keywords: graphical models, gene association networks, partial correlation, microarray data
Poster H05
GraphCrunch: a tool for large network analyses
Milenkovic, T., Lai, J., Przulj, N.
Department of Computer Science, University of California, Irvine
GraphCrunch (http://www.ics.uci.edu/~bio-nets/graphcrunch/) is a software tool that implements the latest research on network analyses and modeling. It is the first tool for comparing large real networks against a series of random network models with respect to a variety of global and local network properties. GraphCrunch is a comprehensive, parallelizable, and easily extendible open-source research software tool for analyzing and modeling of large real-world networks. Our paper describing GraphCrunch has been labeled as Highly Accessed by BMC Bioinformatics where it was published.
Modeling biological networks is a vibrant research area. A good model should generate graphs that resemble real data as closely as possible across a wide range of statistical measures. Testing the efficacy of a model entails comparing model-derived random graphs to real data. GraphCrunch is an open-source software tool that implements the latest research on biological network models and properties: it compares real-world networks against a series of graph models with respect to a variety of network properties. It currently supports five different types of random graphs: Erdos-Renyi random graphs (Erdos and Renyi, 1559), generalized random graphs with the same degree distribution as the real-world network, Barabasi-Albert type scale-free networks (Barabasi and Albert, 1999), n-dimensional geometric random graphs, where n is any positive integer (Penrose, 2003), and stickiness-index-based model networks (Przulj and Higham, 2006). Comparing large networks relies on heuristics, such as global and local network properties, due to NP-completeness of the underlying subgraph isomorphism problem. GraphCrunch currently supports two computationally expensive graphlet-based similarity measures of local network structure, where graphlets are small connected non-isomorphic induced subgraphs of large networks. These two local network properties are the relative graphlet frequency distance, which compares the frequencies of the appearance of graphlets in two networks (Przulj et al., 2004) and the graphlet degree distribution agreement, which is a graphlet-based generalization of the degree distribution (Przulj, 2006). GraphCrunch also computes five standard global network properties: degree distributions, distributions of shortest path lengths, average network diameters, average clustering coefficients, and clustering spectra. GraphCrunch automates the process of generating random networks drawn from user specified random graph models and evaluating the fit of the network models to a real-world network with respect to global and local network properties. In a single command, GraphCrunch performs all of the following tasks: 1) computes user specified global and local properties of an input real-world network, 2) creates a user specified number of random networks belonging to user specified random graph models, 3) compares how closely each model network reproduces a range of global and local properties (specified in point 1 above) of the real-world network, and 4) produces the statistics of network property similarities between the data and the model networks. GraphCrunch is capable of producing these statistics both in tabular and in graphical format. GraphCrunch is the first software tool that compares real-world networks against a series of network models with respect to a variety of local and global network properties. It is easily extendible to include additional network models and properties. GraphCrunch has parallel computing capabilities -- it allows for a user specified list of machines on which to distribute computationally expensive searches for local network properties. This is the first network analysis software that has built-in parallel computing capabilities, a feature that will become crucial as biological network data sets grow. We introduce GraphCrunch as a comprehensive, parallelizable, and easily extendible open-source research software tool for analyzing and modeling large real-world networks. It is freely available at http://www.ics.uci.edu/~bio-nets/graphcrunch/. Although our paper describing GraphCrunch has just recently appeared in BMC Bioinformatics, it has already been labeled as Highly Accessed at the BMC Bioinformatics web-site.
Keywords: protein-protein interaction networks, network analysis and modeling, network analysis software tools, network properties

Random generation of metabolic networks with biochemical constraints
Georg Basler (1), Zoran Nikoloski (1), Oliver Ebenhöh (1,2)
(1) University of Potsdam, Institute for Biochemistry and Biology, Karl-Liebknecht-Str. 24-25, D-14476 Potsdam, Germany, (2) Max-Planck-Institute for Molecular Plant Physiology, Am Mühlenberg 1, D-14476 Potsdam, Germany
Random metabolic networks may be used to determine the statistical significance of biologically relevant structural properties. We present a novel method for randomizing a given metabolic network, which captures the underlying biochemical principle of mass conservation. The method further preserves the salient graph-theoretic properties of metabolic networks and, thus, allows for testing the significance of complex network properties in a biological context.
When analysing the structural properties of metabolic networks, randomly generated networks may be used to determine whether an investigated property is of statistical significance. In order to accurately identify complex properties that have emerged in a biochemically constrained environment, it is necessary for the random network generation to capture as many of the underlying biochemical principles as possible. The existing random methods aim at preserving the graph-theoretic properties of the template network, but do not take into account the biochemical constraints of the metabolic reaction network at hand. We present an approach to the random generation of metabolic networks, which preserves the fundamental biochemical laws of mass conservation. Given a realistic metabolic network, we randomize it by: (1) determining equivalence classes with respect to the conservation of chemical elements and (2) adding reactions to the network by randomly choosing reactants and products from these equivalence classes, while conserving the left and right side chemical elements. Our approach preserves graph-theoretic properties of metabolic networks such as: degree distribution and coupling relationships. We apply the method to the problem of finding the statistical significance of properties related to the biosynthetic potentials of a given metabolic network. Therefore, our method provides a novel contribution to the extraction of biologically relevant information from structural network models.

Pathway analysis improves comparability of different data sets: an application to the study of renal carcinoma
Luca Beltrame (1), Ingrid Cifola (1), Roberta Spinelli (1), Clelia Peano (1), Duccio Cavalieri (2), Cristina Battaglia (3)
(1) Institute of Biomedical Technologies (ITB), National Research Council (CNR), Milan, Italy, (2) Dept. of Pharmacology, University of Florence,Italy, (3) Dept. of Biomedical Science and Technologies, University of Milan,Italy
Microarray technology has been used widely in the study of tumors, to recognize and classify different histological subtypes, to identify differentially expressed genes, or to discover new molecular markers useful for diagnostic and prognostic applications. Unfortunately most gene profiling studies suffered from a lack of overlap and reproducibility with other similar datasets published by different groups. Here we propose an approach aimed at grouping similar microarray samples from different data sets using pathway analysis coupled with standard hierarchical clustering methods.
Microarray technology has been extensively used in the study of tumor pathologies to recognize and classify different histological subtypes, to identify differentially expressed genes, or to discover new molecular markers useful for diagnostic and prognostic applications. Unfortunately, most gene profiling studies suffered from a lack of overlap and reproducibility with other similar datasets published by different groups (Ein-Dor et al., 2005). A solution is the use of "group testing" (also called "pathway analysis") (Manoli et al., 2006) approach, an analysis method that identifies alterations of predefined groups of genes, organized in cellular networks or metabolic pathways, instead of single genes. In order to study the molecular processes involved in renal cell carcinoma (RCC) etiology, we used both a traditional transcriptomic analysis and the novel "pathway analysis" approach, and compared the results obtained with these two methods. To do so, we collected data about RCC samples from three distinct public data sets, with different experimental designs and microarray platforms (Cifola et al., 2008; Zhao et al., 2006; Jones et al., 2005). Using the traditional differential analysis method, the overlap of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) among the three data sets was less than 50%. In order to investigate the similarities between these data sets in terms of disregulated cellular pathways, we used Eu.Gene (Cavalieri et al., 2007), a freely available software, to calculate Enrichment Scores (ES) over a set of more than 1000 pathways and cellular networks applying the Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) algorithm. The ES for each data set were then clustered using standard hierarchical clustering methods. Showing a large similarity between the three data sets, the clustering analysis identified recurrent disregulated pathways common to all data sets and revealed similar expression profiles on pathways such as VEGF, IL-6 and HIF signaling pathways, all important for the development and progression of RCC. The results were similar independently of the experimental design and the microarray platform used. In conclusion, results from traditional whole-genome transcriptomic analysis on different data sets overlap poorly, so causing difficulty in the extraction of reliable and biologically meaningful results. Differently, the novel "pathway analysis" approach improves the comparability of different data sets and microarray platforms, thus aiding the interpretation of the results and the study of complex biological systems. Integrative Analysis of the Oct-4 Regulatory Network in hESCs
Chavez L., Adjaye J., Lehrach H., Herwig R.
Max-Planck-Institute for Molecular Genetics, Department of Vertebrate Genomics, Ihnestrasse 73, 14195 Berlin, Germany.
Pluripotency and self-renewing is controlled by a transcription regulatory network governed by Oct4 regulation and the down-regulation of Oct4 is a crucial initiating for early embryonic differentiation. We have carried out a systematic survey of available experiments for analyzing transcriptional dependencies as ChIP-on-Chip and RNAi along with sequence based analysis of the promoter regions of putative target genes in order to identify a core Oct4 regulatory network.
The transcription factor Oct4 is highly expressed in the inner cell mass of the human blastocyst and recent studies on reprogramming human somatic cells to induced pluripotent stem cells emphasize its function as a key regulator on top of a transcriptional regulatory network controlling the maintanance of pluripotency in hESCs. It is supposed, that pluripotency and self-renewing is controlled by a transcription regulatory network governed by Oct4 regulation and the down-regulation of Oct4 is a crucial initiating for early embryonic differentiation. Several studies employed high-throughput experiments such as ChIP-on-Chip and RNAi in order to identify direct targets of Oct4 transcriptional regulation. We have carried out a systematic survey of these experiments along with sequence analysis of the promoter regions of putative target genes in order to identify a core regulatory network. Direct target genes of Oct4 and of the transcription factors Sox2 and Nanog were already identified in hESCs by ChIP-on-Chip experiments. For obtaining a more stringent set of DNA-Protein interactions, the available ChIP-on-Chip data was quality controlled and a subset of target genes was isolated by an additional analysis. The information about validated direct target genes was complemented by the results taken from a study that examined global transcriptional changes after RNAi-mediated suppression of the Oct4 function in hESCs. Thirdly, transcriptional dependencies were analyzed by a sequence based approach. Here, experimental identified DNA-Protein interactions were validated by mapping known Oct4 related motifs to the promotor regions of potential target genes. Functional annotation analysis revealed that the enrichment of genes connected to pluripotency and differentiation increases by integrating the results of the individual studies. Finally, a core transcriptional regulatory network was constructed composed of the core Oct4 target genes. The network highlighting putative key regulators and transcription factors controlling the maintance of pluripotency as well as genes involved in the process of differentiation that are either activated or suppressed by Oct4 in hESCs.

Clancy T, Hovig E

Pathway Alignment: A Comparative Approach in Understanding Metabolic Evolution While the genome may be the blue-print of a living organism, it is the well coordinated metabolic system that actually keeps it alive. To better understand the numerous complex cascades of enzyme substrate interactions that provide every living organism with their “vital force”, a thorough analysis of the structural design and capabilities of the cellular metabolic networks is necessary. The current work proposes an efficient model for metabolic network analysis with a directed effort to study the evolution of metabolic complexity from the analysed data. The metabolic data - enzymes, metabolites and reactions, are primarily collected from the KEGG Pathway database (Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes). For the sake of simplicity and also due the paucity of data in current public databases, kinetic parameters for the reactions are not considered for this stage of the work. The pathways are then mapped with the help of Graph theory into a connected network of nodes and edges. This can be done in two ways, (i) the nodes may represent the metabolites while the edges represent the enzymes driving the reactions, or (ii) the nodes being the enzymes (centres of reaction) while the edges denoting the metabolite ‘flux’ entering or leaving the nodes. The “labeled graph” is then transformed onto corresponding adjacency and incidence matrices for better mathematical handling. These matrices may then be used to predict shortest path between metabolites and also for subgraph matching of short portions of pathways, as have been previously shown by some researchers (Pinter et. al., 2005, Bioinformatics). However, this work focuses on generating such matrices for a particular pathway from different organisms, preferably from different taxonomic levels, and comparing them. Graph alignment is a more challenging task than simple sequence alignment which may be done in polynomial time using dynamic programming algorithms. Therefore, for this work a strategy quite similar to the local graph alignment algorithm as described by Berg and Lassig (2004, PNAS) has been adapted. The algorithm is based upon efficient heuristic search logic where graph alignment is mapped onto a “spin model” familiar in statistical physics. This combined with the earlier methods of finding “largest common subgraphs” and re- occurring motifs, gives a fairly efficient platform to compare between metabolic networks from different organisms and imply evolutionary relationships. To start with, the model has been tested on some common metabolic pathways from 10 different microbes and a phylogeny has been reconstructed based on alignment scores from the metabolic data. Although analyses made on very few pathways from a few organisms are not rewarding enough data to conclude anything from the evolutionary viewpoint, it is hoped that future efforts encompassing more number of pathways (on a full metabolic scale, if possible), will help us to understand the evolution of metabolic complexity. The data generated may not only be useful for the emerging field of metabolic engineering, but also for designing new drugs by studying the “choke points” from the network topology (Rahman and Schomburg, 2006, Bioinformatics).

The theory of co-evolution of protein families states that interacting protein families tend to have similar phylogenetic trees. These similarities can be used to predict the mapping between two families of interacting proteins (i.e. which proteins from one family interact with which members of the other). The correct mapping will be the one that maximizes the similarity between the two trees. Moreover, additional a-priori information can be used to improve the results. For example, provided these two families comprise orthologs and paralogs, this is, members of the two families are present in more than one organism, this fact can be exploited to restrict the possible mappings, simply by impeding links between proteins of different organisms. We present here TAG_TSEMA, a server implementing an algorithm to predict the mapping between families of interacting proteins which is able to incorporate information regarding orthologs, or any other assignment of proteins to "classes" that may restrict possible mappings in order to improve the results. The method accurately predicts around 80% in the most favorable cases on a large number of interacting protein domains. We also analyzed in detail the results of the method for a well defined case of interacting families, the sensor and kinase components of the Ntr-type two-component system, for which up to 98% of the pairings predicted by the method were correct. Because we are interested in the finding of the subnetworks in the initial network, and not in the network obtained because of the change of the connectivity matrix, the value of Q is calculated according to the values of the initial connectivity matrix. The division which we accept as a proper one is the one with the highest value of the modularity. In many cases this approach works better than the GN-algorithm (3). Bacteria's can resist to antibiotic either by (i) chemical modification of antibiotic, (ii) play it inactive through physical removal from cell (efflux mechanism) or (iii) modify target site (by mutation) so that it is not recognized by the antibiotic. A fundamental understanding of transport mechanism might aid to future design of antibiotics with an improved permeation properties so as to tackle the problem of bacterial resistance. General diffusion porins (such as OmpF, OmpC) present in large abundance in the cell membrane form the main pathway for diffusion of Betalactam antibiotics. Recent Electrophysiology (BLM) experiments have pointed out that the diffusion of antibiotics through the Outer membrane protein (OmpF) is controlled mainly by interactions at the molecular scale. We find the free energy surface to change drastically for the mutants with respect to that of wild type. Our simulation results are in good agreement with BLM and liposome swelling assay experiments. Moreover, we further extended this investigation to carbenicillin (charged -2) and Cephalosporins, (fourth generation antibiotics) which are the most frequently prescribed class of antibiotics in the world. Summing up we propose for the antibiotics in study a reaction pathway for the translocation process , with an effort to rationalize with the experimental findings.

Proteins are essential macromolecules of life and understanding their function is important. The number of functionally unclassified proteins is large even for simple and well studied organisms such as yeast. Methods for determining protein function have shifted their focus from targeting specific proteins based solely on sequence homology to analyses of the entire proteome using protein-protein interaction (PPI) networks. PPI networks are commonly represented as graphs in which nodes correspond to proteins and undirected edges represent physical interactions between them. Since proteins aggregate to perform a certain function, PPI networks by definition reflect the interconnected nature of biological processes. Therefore, analyzing structural properties of PPI networks may provide useful clues about the function of individual proteins, protein complexes, and even larger cellular machines. Defining the relationship between the PPI network topology and biological function and determining protein function from it is one of the most challenging problems in the post-genomic era. The method we propose addresses this challenge. First, we use it to demonstrate that in numerous PPI networks of yeast and human, biological function and local network structure are closely related, by successfully grouping topologically similar proteins belonging to the same complex, functional, subcellular localization, and tissue expression categories. Next, we apply this technique to infer biological function of yet unclassified proteins in yeast. Our method generalizes the degree of a node, which measures the number of edges that the node touches, into the vector of additional 72 graphlet degrees, measuring the number of graphlets that the node touches, where graphlets are small connected non-isomorphic induced subgraphs of a large network (Przulj et al., 2004). Clearly, the degree of a node is the first one in this vector, since an edge is the only 2-node graphlet. Thus, for each node we generate this vector and we call it the signature of the node. We design a signature-based node similarity measure as a number in [0, 1], where 1 means the identity between the signatures of two nodes. We call this measure the node signature similarity. For a node of interest, we form a cluster containing that node, and all nodes in a network that are similar to it. We define two nodes to be similar if their signature similarity is above a certain threshold. Next, we identify the common protein complex, functional, subcellular localization, and tissue expression categories that nodes in each cluster belong to. Ideally, we expect to find a category common to all nodes in a cluster. We demonstrate that our method is capable of identifying groups of topologically similar nodes that share common biological properties. Thus, our technique can be applied to identify biological properties of yet unclassified proteins by forming a cluster of proteins that are similar to the unclassified protein of interest and assigning it the most common biological properties of the classified proteins in the cluster. We validate our predictions in the literature. We present proteome-scale network-topology-based method for detecting protein complexes, function, subcellular localization, and tissue expression. Additionally, it can be used to infer other biological properties. Moreover, the method can be applied to PPI networks of other organisms as well as to other types of biological networks. Our method can give insight into complex biological mechanisms and provide valuable guidelines for the future experimental research.

(1) Massachusetts Institute of Technology, (2) University of Catania
Developing complex models of cellular functions requires the collaboration of multiple teams of researchers remotely distributed worldwide. A challenge of computational systems biology is to find easy and accessible mechanisms to enable such collaboration. This poster presents the development of an on-line web portal for enabling open access to Cytosolve, an existing, proven and scalable computational architecture for integrating quantitative molecular pathways. A grand challenge of Systems Biology is to model the whole cell. A cell consists of a set of organelles that interact to provide cellular functions such as protein synthesis, metabolism, apoptosis, or motility. Systems Biology aims to develop a model of the cell by connecting the biochemical kinetics of these interactions at the molecular mechanistic level to derive the quantitative descriptions of higher-level cellular functions. There is a worldwide movement in the computational systems biology community to find powerful ways to integrate the growing number of biological pathway models. The open-access on-line web portal discussed in this poster is based on the scalable computational architecture of Cytosolve, which allows the integration of an ensemble of distributed biological pathway models [Ayyadurai, PhD. Thesis, 2007]. The Cytosolve architecture has been validated on well-known biological models in the systems biology community by comparing solutions obtained with the existing approaches. Moreover, new integrative models, such as the interferon (IFN) response to virus infection, were developed using Cytosolve. Cytosolve architecture [Ayyadurai and Dewey, BMC Bioinformatics, 2008] directly addresses the integration and scalability problems of coupling multiple biological pathways by providing a parallel and distributed architecture. The computational architecture used by Cytosolve is based on the dynamic messaging approach, which implies that the models remain independently executing programs that interact only by exchanging data via message passing during execution. This kind of architecture removes the need to manually load, understand and interconnect each individual pathway into a single monolithic program as is required in other systems. The logical software architecture of Cytosolve provides a Controller, a Presentation and Communications Layer that includes a Web Server with its Graphical User Interface and the Web Services, and finally the Remote Node machines. The Controller mediates the execution and messaging between the models. It consists of three main components: the Monitor that serves to track the progress of each pathway’s solution time, the Communications Manager (Comm Mgr) and the Mass Balance algorithm. The Controller serves two purposes. First, by means of the Comm Mgr, it mediates communication across all pathway models. Second, using the Mass Balance algorithm, it provides computational steering by ensuring mass conservation across all integrated models for each time step. The Web Services are designed for remote model simulations. During a run, the web service can be instructed by a remote computer to perform two major operations. First, the web service can be instructed to simulate a local model over a single time step. After the simulation, the service sends back new concentration values calculated by the model. In the second operation, the web service can be instructed to insert new species concentration values into the model simulation. This allows external control of the simulation. The model simulator on the Remote Node machines is the SBML ODE Solver Library (SOSlib) [Machné et al., 2006] but other solvers supporting CellML, MML, and other pathway description dialects can be used interchangeably with SOSlib. The Graphical User Interface (GUI) was developed by means of the JavaServer Pages (JSP) technology. JSP are supported with Java Server Faces (JSF) that provides tag libraries with custom actions for representing the standard UI components in the JSP pages. A User from a Remote Node machine, by means of the GUI and the interaction with the Web Server, can: 1. Single model local simulation; 2. Single model remote simulation; 3. Integration of multiple models simulation (local and remote). A remote user by means of the web GUI is driven step by step on the simulation. When the topology of simulation is chosen, the user will be asked to submit the model (or the models in the case of multiple simulations) that should be a file resident on the local machine, a URL linking to a specific model or a model inside the database on the remote server. The local simulation requires that a solver is installed on the machine, if it is not available, the user will be asked to download it. The local solver is also needed when a multiple simulation is chosen and the user wants to simulate a model locally coupling the simulation with the other remote node machines on the network. After the setting of the parameters for each model, the simulation can be executed and the results will be displayed and stored. The local simulation of the model implies a use of the local computational resources of the Remote Node machines but on the other hand it ensures protection of the proprietary models. Many pharmaceutical companies, for example, will not want to share the inner source code of their particular proprietary models; however, they are interested in coupling their models with other models to gain better understanding of a larger cellular process. On the other hand the black-box simulations of the models allow researchers to integrate Public models with existing Proprietary models to learn some new aspect of science, without violating confidentiality issues. In summary, the main features of the on-line GUI-based web portal to Cytosolve presented in this poster are: - Ease of access to the Cytosolve distributed and scalable architecture. The architecture is able to integrate new pathway models with the same ease as it is to integrate the first one. - Multiple platform availability for the simulations. The computational system allows models developed on different hardware and computing environments. - Open accessibility. The architecture and the implementation of the web application support protocols for communicating with models anywhere without regard to geographical location.
